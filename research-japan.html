Bridging Cultures

By Md Athar Eshtiyaque Enab

Introduction

In recent years, researchers have been enthusiastic in promoting the integration of native English-speaking teachers (NESTs) as a powerful catalyst for the development of a collaborative online and offline learning environment to support professional learning during school placement (Wenger, 1998; Schlager et al., 2002; Gómez and Rico, 2007; Kirschner and Lai, 2007). From a teacher’s perspective, I believe that professional learning is important in a desire to establish a sense of belonging and contribution to the community in which knowledge and practice are fundamental community property (Lieberman and Pointer-Mace, 2008). This sense of belonging and contributing to a community appears particularly meaningful for me as I’m wanting to be a teacher. Because the person I’m researching on was geographically and culturally isolated from his other peers and family members in Northern Ireland during the course of his five-years (2019-2024) of teaching in Japan, a collaborative and accessible venue to share and discuss practice, eliminate doubts, and seek support might have better motivated and engaged him in my professional growth (Lieberman and Pointer-Mace, 2008). This particular assignment seeks to look for one of my classmates’ real-life experiences as a teacher from Northern Ireland working within the Japanese school system.

Research Question

How does a Northern Irish native English speaker acquire professional identity and pedagogical adaptation during a five-year teaching period in Japan?

Key Themes

  • Professional Identity
  • Pedagogical Adaptation
  • Peer Interaction and Cultural Behaviour
  • Relationship Building
  • Reflection and Professional Growth

Findings

The pilot testing phase proved to be an invaluable exercise in identifying the linguistic and conceptual barriers within the instrument. One of the most significant findings was that my initial questions were heavily laden with Western educational jargon that did not translate well to the specific experience of teaching in Japan. For instance, the original question (question 4), "Which factors motivated you to join a school in Japan as an English teacher?" was flagged as "really big topic" as the interviewee said, “'factors' is a really big topic, and it's hard to know how much to write”. The pilot respondent noted that “Maybe asking for just the biggest factor would be better”. Consequently, I re-wrote this phrase and simplified the prompt to: " What is the biggest factor that motivated you to join a school in Japan as an English teacher?" This revision immediately made the question more accessible and elicited a much deeper narrative response regarding the teacher's genuine intentions behind joining the teaching program in Japan. Furthermore, the pilot revealed a critical flaw in the sequencing of questions. Initially, I placed sensitive questions regarding "losing face" (the Japanese concept of Menboku) and professional failures at the very beginning. The pilot respondent indicated that answering these high-stress questions immediately made them feel defensive, which limited the honesty of their subsequent answers. Based on this, I reordered the online form to start with "gentle lead-in" questions regarding general background and positive experiences before moving into the more complex issues of cultural conflict. Additionally, I found that I had neglected the nuance of the "Northern Irish" element in my first draft. The pilot respondent, who is from a UK background, noted that "British" or "English-speaking" was too broad a category. To fix this, I added a specific section titled "Regional Identity," which allowed me to probe how specific Northern Irish traits, such as a specific type of self-deprecating humor functioned in a Japanese context. I also increased the character limit on the text boxes. The pilot showed that when provided with a small box, respondents gave "bullet-point" answers. By expanding the digital field size and using prompts like " How was your overall experience as a foreign language teacher in Japan?”, I successfully encouraged the kind of rich, thick description that is the hallmark of qualitative research (Patton, 1990; Robson, 2002). Finally, I included a "Save and Continue" feature on the online form, as the pilot respondent mentioned that the deep reflection required for these questions was mentally taxing and required more than one sitting to complete thoughtfully.

Conclusion

The research demonstrates that teaching abroad involves more than classroom practice. Professional growth emerges through navigating cultural differences, adapting teaching methods, building relationships, and developing cultural competence within a new educational environment.
